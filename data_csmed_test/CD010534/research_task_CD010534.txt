Included studies: Comparisons of two therapeutic modalities, parent-infant psychotherapies/dyadic interventions targeting attachment issues, studies involving parent-child attachment dyads with specific attention to child functioning (e.g., attachment or dysregulation problems), and studies targeting infants up to 24 months old in non-foster care settings. Excluded studies: Stand-alone video-interaction guidance interventions not incorporating additional components.  
other_info: The protocol for evaluating PIP effectiveness was published (Barlow 2013). The review included both RCTs and quasi-RCTs, focusing on parent-infant psychotherapies. Studies targeting attachment issues in parent-child dyads were prioritized, with a focus on improving infant attachment security and socio-emotional functioning through parent-infant interactions. Additional components were included if they met the core criteria. A total of 2604 results were retrieved across all databases, reduced to 1921 after de-duplication. Trials involved parents or mothers receiving psychotherapy for infants, focusing on areas like attachment issues, secure bonding, and parent-child relationships. The study covered a wide range of topics within child and family therapy, emphasizing the importance of psychotherapy in early intervention. Challenges included data limitations due to reliance on public sources, which may have excluded some studies, affecting comprehensiveness. Methodological gaps such as not drawing funnel plots due to insufficient study numbers were noted, highlighting limitations in assessing publication bias. The study successfully gathered significant data from multiple sources, providing valuable insights into psychotherapy interventions for infants despite challenges related to data scope and methodology.

other_info: The protocol for evaluating the effectiveness of attachment-based interventions was published (Barlow 2013). This review specifically targeted mother-infant psychotherapies and attachment-based interventions, focusing on improving sensitive discipline in mothers of young children at risk for externalizing behavior problems. Studies prioritized those targeting attachment issues within parent-child dyads, with a specific focus on enhancing secure bonding and attachment security to improve child's socio-emotional functioning and externalizing behaviors. Additional components were included if they met the core criteria. A total of 2604 results were retrieved across all databases, reduced to 1921 after de-duplication. Trials involved parents or mothers receiving psychotherapy for children at risk, focusing on attachment-based interventions such as secure bonding training, attachment therapy, and parent-child interaction training. The study covered a wide range of attachment-based approaches within child and family therapy, emphasizing the importance of attachment-based interventions in early intervention. Challenges included data limitations due to reliance on public sources, which may have excluded some studies, affecting comprehensiveness. Methodological gaps such as not drawing funnel plots due to insufficient study numbers were noted, highlighting limitations in assessing publication bias. The study successfully gathered significant data from multiple sources, providing valuable insights into attachment-based psychotherapy interventions for improving sensitive discipline in at-risk mothers despite challenges related to data scope and methodology.
