Rosuvastatin research focuses on its effects in lowering LDL-C (low-density lipoprotein cholesterol) and HDL-C (high-density lipoprotein cholesterol). The study employs multiple databases, including CENTRAL, MEDLINE, EMBASE, Web of Science, and BIOSIS, with a search conducted between 2013 and 2014. Key findings indicate that Rosuvastatin demonstrates significant dose-dependent effects, particularly in reducing LDL-C and HDL-C levels. Detailed analysis of changes across different doses is conducted, along with consideration of end-of-treatment variability and adverse event-related withdrawals. The research adheres to a three-to-twelve week trial duration, ensuring consistent administration of Rosuvastatin at the same daily dose compared to placebo or alone during this period. Key findings suggest that Rosuvastatin may be more potent than Atorvastatin in lipid-lowering effects. The study design includes randomised placebo-controlled trials (RCTs) and before-and-after studies, with participants being any age, with or without cardiovascular disease, normal or abnormal lipid profiles, and individuals with various comorbidities. Participants must stabilise their sertraline diet prior to treatment and avoid all medications or supplements that may affect lipid metabolism during the washout period. The primary outcome measures include changes in LDL, HDL, non-HDL cholesterol, total cholesterol, and triglyceride levels. Additionally, standard deviations and coefficient of variations at endpoints are recorded as important metrics. Research also focuses on adverse event-related withdrawals (WDAEs) as an important endpoint. Details of the search strategy include the use of specific keywords such as rosuvastatin, crestor, s4522, zd4522, with a focus on non-human research exclusion.

Additionally, a new study titled "A Randomized Controlled Study to Compare the Effects of Rosuvastatin 5 mg and Atorvastatin 10 mg on the Plasma Lipid Profile in Japanese Patients with Hypercholesterolemia (ASTRO-2)" has been identified. This study compares the lipid-lowering effects of Rosuvastatin at a lower dose (5mg) versus Atorvastatin at a higher dose (10mg) specifically among Japanese patients with hypercholesterolemia. The results may provide additional insights into the comparative efficacy and safety profiles of these two statins in a diverse patient population, potentially informing clinical decision-making and treatment optimization strategies for hyperlipidemia management.
