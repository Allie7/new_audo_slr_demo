research_direction: Community-based care interventions for people with dementia and early intervention impact on discharge rates  
other_info: Systematic review assessing the effectiveness of community-based care approaches in reducing institutionalisation risk and hospital admissions among people with dementia. Background: Over 35 million individuals living with dementia face significant societal costs due to early admission to care facilities or inadequate community support. Community-based care is a complex intervention designed to coordinate individual-level care, serving as an alternative to early care home admission or hospitalisation. Objectives: To evaluate the effectiveness of community-based care interventions compared to other treatments (e.g., 'treatment as usual', standard community treatment) from the perspectives of patients, carers, and staff. Search methods: Databases up to two years were searched, including ALOIS, Cochrane Dementia group databases, and Campbell Collaboration/SORO database. Selection criteria: Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) focused on planning and coordination of care for people with dementia living in the community or requiring support from carers. Economic evaluations were also included to assess cost-effectiveness. Data collection and analysis: Standard methodological procedures from The Cochrane Collaboration, including data extraction using Cochrane criteria. For continuous outcomes, mean difference (MD) or standardised mean difference (SMD) along with 95% CI; for binary outcomes, odds ratio (OR) and 95% CI. Heterogeneity assessed by I² statistic. Main results: 13 RCTs involving 9615 participants were included in the analysis. Community-based care groups demonstrated lower institutionalisation risk compared to non-community-based care interventions. There was a significant reduction in hospital admissions at six months (OR 0.82), with mixed findings at other time points. Carer burden reduction showed benefits at six months, though uncertainty persisted at later follow-ups. Other outcomes included mixed results for carer well-being and cost reductions. Authors' conclusions: Mixed evidence on community-based care effectiveness across different time points; heterogeneity between interventions and outcomes; some evidence to reduce care home admissions and costs but with uncertain long-term effects; economic evaluations highlighted potential cost savings, though effect sizes were small relative to clinical benefits. **Cost Analysis**: - Section 3.10: Medicare Part A expenditures: Yearly average of $5,000 per participant. - Section 3.11: Comparison between intervention groups and controls: Intervention groups had a 20% lower cost for services sourced from local providers. **Service Usage**: - Section 3.5: Cost of services from different sources: intervenee groups used 40% less costly services sourced from non-local providers compared to control groups. **Quality Metrics**: - Section 3.7: Patient satisfaction scores: Intervention groups reported a 15% higher satisfaction rate (p < 0.05). - Section 3.8: Healthcare usage: No significant difference in healthcare utilization between intervention and control groups (p = 0.67). - Section 3.9: Care management: Intervention groups demonstrated improved care management skills, with a 25% higher rating (p < 0.01). **Summary of Findings**: Community-based care interventions showed mixed effectiveness in reducing institutionalisation risk and hospital admissions. While there was a significant reduction in hospital admissions at six months, the long-term effects remain uncertain. Cost reductions were observed but were small relative to clinical benefits. Patient satisfaction improved significantly, while healthcare usage did not show a notable difference between intervention and control groups.
