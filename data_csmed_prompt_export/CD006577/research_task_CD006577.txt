other_info: A systematic review was conducted to evaluate the efficacy of music therapy interventions for individuals undergoing coronary heart disease (CHD) procedures, such as angiography. The review included all randomized clinical trials and quasi-randomized controlled trials in any language, published and unpublished, that met the following criteria: inclusion of male, female, and child participants; inpatient or outpatient setting; confirmed CHD diagnosis. Studies involving participants who did not have a confirmed CHD diagnosis (e.g., individuals undergoing cardiac diagnostic procedures) were excluded from this update.  

The review assessed the impact of music interventions on various outcomes, including psychological distress such as anxiety and depression, quality of life, physiological responses like heart rate and blood pressure, and pain management. The primary outcomes measured were psychological distress and quality of life, while secondary outcomes included cardiovascular parameters (e.g., systolic and diastolic blood pressure) and pain assessment. All outcome measures were prioritized based on the availability of validated instruments for measurement. Primary outcomes were self-reported by participants, whereas physiological responses were recorded by observers who may or may not have been blinded to the intervention type.  

The review found that music therapy interventions demonstrated small but statistically significant improvements in anxiety reduction among CHD patients, particularly those with myocardial infarction (STEMI). Listening to music was associated with reductions in heart rate, respiratory rate, and systolic blood pressure. Quality of life measures showed modest improvements in participants' overall well-being. However, the clinical significance of these findings remains under further investigation due to limited sample sizes and variability in outcome assessments across studies.  

Additionally, a non-randomized controlled trial examined the effectiveness of music therapy for elderly patients with cerebrovascular disease and dementia who experienced high anxiety during coronary angiographic procedures. Participants who listened to music during the procedure reported lower levels of anxiety compared to those who did not. This study highlights the potential benefits of incorporating music therapy into pre-intervention stress management programs for high-risk patients undergoing cardiac procedures.
